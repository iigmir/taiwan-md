# Taiwan: The Fruit Kingdom's Sweet and Bitter Reality

> **30-second overview:** Taiwan's mango industry generates about NT$7.6 billion annually, with every other mango in Taiwan coming from Tainan. But in 2021, a political storm caused sugar apples—90% dependent on the Chinese market—to lose their outlet overnight. This reveals the true struggles of Taiwan's fruit industry: climate change threats, aging workforce, and over-concentrated export risks.

In the sweltering summer heat, a single Yujing Irwin mango can cost over NT$100 per pound (about $3.50). That's enough to buy breakfast for three days. But farmers say: "We don't want to charge this much—Mother Nature just isn't cooperating."

In May 2025, extreme weather severely reduced Taiwan's mango harvest. At the same time, sugar apple farmers who once depended on the Chinese market were still struggling with sudden import bans from 2021. One side faces natural disasters, the other political interference. Taiwan's so-called "fruit kingdom" is facing unprecedented challenges.

## The Truth Behind Yujing's Mango Empire

When Taiwanese think of mangoes, Yujing inevitably comes to mind. Roughly every other mango in Taiwan comes from Tainan, with Yujing serving as the capital of this mango empire.

But Yujing's mango dominance wasn't built overnight. Irwin mangoes were first introduced from Florida by Kaohsiung County Agricultural Association in 1954. After years of cultivation, they found their ideal growing environment in Tainan's mountainous regions. Yujing's elevation of 200-300 meters, significant day-night temperature variations, and well-drained red soil created the unique sweet flavor of Irwin mangoes.

The numbers speak for themselves: Taiwan cultivates mangoes across 15,000 hectares, producing 153,000 tons annually with a value of approximately NT$7.6 billion. Tainan accounts for half of Taiwan's total production, with Yujing District serving as the core production area.

Behind the glory lies harsh reality. Mr. Chen, a veteran Yujing farmer, explains: "An Irwin mango can sell for over NT$100 now, but that's not good news. The high price reflects nearly 50% production loss."

Climate change is the greatest enemy. Spring 2025's abnormal high temperatures disrupted mango flowering cycles, while May's continuous heavy rains damaged nearly-ripe fruits. Production costs haven't decreased—labor, fertilizer, and pesticides have all increased—yet yields become increasingly unstable.

## The Shattered Chinese Dream of Sugar Apples

If mangoes face natural disasters, sugar apples (custard apples) face man-made ones.

Taiwan began exporting sugar apples in 2003, targeting the Chinese market. After more than a decade of cultivation, China accounted for 99.4% of Taiwan's sugar apple exports by 2016. Taitung farmers called this complete dependence on a single market "putting all eggs in one basket."

On September 20, 2021, China suddenly announced a ban on Taiwan's fresh sugar apple imports, citing "pest detection." Overnight, 90% of export-bound sugar apples lost their market. During peak harvest season, farmers watched their carefully cultivated "golden fruit" rot on trees.

The Council of Agriculture urgently announced plans to redirect 5,000 tons of sugar apples to Japan, Hong Kong, Malaysia, and other markets. But reality proved harsh: sugar apples don't tolerate low temperatures well, requiring 7-10 days for sea transport, often arriving in Japan soft and spoiled. Moreover, Japanese consumers, accustomed to "elegantly eaten" fruits, showed low acceptance for seed-spitting sugar apples.

The General Manager of Taitung Sugar Apple Farmers' Association explained: "Sugar apples sell for 1,000 yen (about NT$300) each in Japan, but the market is too small to absorb our production volume."

## Labor Crisis: No Young People Want to Farm

Beyond natural disasters and political risks, Taiwan's fruit farmers face another reality: no one wants to inherit their work.

Walking through any orchard reveals predominantly elderly farmers. According to Ministry of Agriculture statistics, Taiwan's farmers average over 62 years old—better than Japan's 67, but the trend is concerning.

Why don't young people want to farm? Second-generation farmer Xiao Lin from Yujing explains: "After university, I worked in Taipei earning NT$50,000 monthly. Returning to grow mangoes, making NT$1 million annually is impressive, but that million comes from 365 days without holidays. When typhoons come, we rush to harvest; when pests appear, we spray pesticides. Where are the weekends?"

Labor shortages directly increase costs. Workers who once cost NT$200 per day now demand NT$300-400 and still aren't guaranteed. During harvest season, farmers must import migrant workers from Vietnam and Indonesia.

Mechanization offers one solution, but Taiwan's orchards are mostly on hillsides with rugged terrain unsuitable for large machinery. Government-promoted "smart agriculture" sounds appealing, but for small farmers, an automated irrigation system costing NT$500,000 is unaffordable.

## Export Dilemma: All Eggs in One Basket

Taiwan fruit export problems extend beyond sugar apples. Over the past decade, China has been Taiwan's most important fresh fruit export market, with annual purchases ranging from NT$1-4 billion. Besides sugar apples, pineapples and wax apples also heavily depend on Chinese markets.

This over-concentration risk exploded in 2021. In March, China banned Taiwan pineapple imports; in September, it banned sugar apples and wax apples. All cited "pest detection," but the industry knew this was political manipulation.

The government announced "market diversification," but it's easier said than done. While Japanese markets offer high prices, they maintain strict quarantine standards and extremely high appearance requirements. Southeast Asian markets have limited purchasing power and their own tropical fruit production bases. European and American markets are distant, with high transportation costs.

For Taiwan fruits to truly "go global," they need more than government policy support—they require comprehensive industry upgrades: quality standardization, brand marketing, cold chain logistics, each requiring massive investment.

## The Long-term Threat of Climate Change

While political risks create short-term impacts, climate change poses long-term threats.

Ministry of Agriculture reports indicate rising temperatures may shift Taiwan's fruit cultivation zones, advance or delay harvest periods, and reduce fruit quality. More frightening is increased extreme weather: super typhoons, record-breaking rainfall, and unprecedented high temperatures all devastate fruit trees.

Legislative Yuan analysis shows natural disaster agricultural losses averaged NT$12.4 billion annually over 15 years, reaching NT$38.34 billion in 2016. Behind these figures lie countless farmers' blood, sweat, and tears.

Traditional disaster compensation can no longer address such challenges. Government-promoted agricultural insurance faces high premiums and strict claim conditions, with low small-farmer participation. Many farmers prefer gambling on luck over paying insurance premiums.

## Breaking Through: Quality and Innovation

Facing multiple challenges, Taiwan farmers haven't sat idle.

In quality improvement, more farmers adopt "premium strategies." Taitung sugar apple farmers switching to organic cultivation sell individual fruits for NT$300; Pingtung wax apple farmers developing "black pearl" varieties—with purple-black skin and extreme sweetness—have become market favorites.

In marketing innovation, many farmers livestream fruit sales, bypassing middlemen to reach consumers directly. During the pandemic, online delivery became a new trend, with packaging-savvy farmers earning more than before.

In technological innovation, facility agriculture is spreading. Despite large initial investments, it allows environmental control and reduces natural disaster risks. Some large orchards introduce greenhouse cultivation, automatic irrigation, and pest monitoring systems.

## Fruit Kingdom at the Crossroads

Taiwan's fruit industry now stands at a crossroads. Going left might mean wallowing in past glory, continuing traditional production methods and single-market dependence. Going right requires facing transformation pain and uncertainty.

Government roles are important but not omnipotent. Industrial policies need long-term planning, not sudden turns due to short-term political storms. Farmers need stable policy environments allowing confident investment and long-term planning.

Consumer support is also crucial. Taiwan fruits maintain excellent quality but relatively high prices. If consumers willingly pay reasonable prices for quality, they can support farmers' continued improvements.

Most importantly, Taiwan's fruit industry needs to shift from "quantity" competition to "quality" competition. Rather than competing with Southeast Asian countries on volume, focus on quality, safety, and branding. Taiwan's fruit technology and quality control capabilities remain world-class.

## Not Just Sweetness, But Resilience

Today, when we bite into sweet Taiwan fruit, we taste not just fruit sweetness, but farmers' resilience. Under climate change threats, market competition pressures, and labor shortage difficulties, Taiwan farmers persist in growing world-class fruit.

Taiwan's "fruit kingdom" title comes not from largest production volumes but highest quality. Behind this title stands generations of farmers' persistence and innovation. As long as this persistence continues, Taiwan fruits will keep shining on the world stage.
