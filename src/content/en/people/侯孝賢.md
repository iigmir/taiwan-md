# Hou Hsiao-hsien

> **30-Second Overview**
>
> A director who refused close-ups and never asked actors to memorize lines, yet became the world's most influential Chinese-language filmmaker. Hou Hsiao-hsien (1947-) revolutionized cinema with his "landscape painting-like long-take aesthetics." His 1989 film *A City of Sadness* won the Golden Lion at Venice, launching Taiwan cinema's international golden age. His influence extends to contemporary masters like Jia Zhangke and Hirokazu Kore-eda, proving that "anti-Hollywood" filmmaking can create eternal art. After winning Best Director at Cannes for *The Assassin* in 2015, he retired in 2023 due to Alzheimer's disease, ending a legendary career.

In 1988, outside the iron gates of the Venice Film Festival, a group of Taiwanese directors gazed longingly at the entrance to this cinematic temple, unsure when they might enter. Among them was Hou Hsiao-hsien, 41, already an award winner at European second-tier festivals but still mocked by Taiwan media as a winner of "village festivals."

One year later, inside those same gates, he held the Golden Lion for *A City of Sadness*—Taiwan cinema's first top prize at an international A-list festival. Even more remarkably, this "incomprehensible" film earned NT$60 million at the box office, with *City of Sadness* program booklets flying off the shelves at Taipei Station bookstores.

From being dismissed as "box office poison" to conquering Venice, Hou Hsiao-hsien used the most anti-mainstream methods—rejecting close-ups, refusing to accommodate cameras, rejecting traditional dramaturgy—to create cinema history's most unique visual language.

## From Military Dependents' Village to Venice: A Hakka Boy's Cinema Journey

### Origins of Crossing (1947-1969)

Born April 8, 1947, in Meixian, Guangdong, to a Hakka family, Hou Hsiao-hsien moved with his family to Fengshan military dependents' village in Kaohsiung at age one. Growing up in this environment where Mainlander and Taiwanese cultures intermingled, "crossing" became his creative DNA—crossing not only provincial origins but eras, languages, and memories.

> "The military village experience taught me early that there's nothing pure in this world." —Hou Hsiao-hsien

His naturally introverted, observant personality made him a born "silent gazer." Academically average in high school, he preferred novels and music. In 1969, he entered the Cinema Department of National Taiwan Academy of Arts (now National Taiwan University of Arts), formally entering the film world.

### Commercial Director Formation (1980-1983)

At 33, Hou began directing in 1980. His first three films—*Cute Girl*, *Cheerful Wind*, *Green, Green Grass of Home*—were commercially successful romantic comedies: fresh, sweet, perfectly meeting market expectations.

But this period already contained seeds of future revolution: he began questioning "Why must films be made this way?"

### The New Cinema Calling (1983-1989)

In 1982, Xiao Ye and Wu Nian-jen organized *In Our Time* and *The Sandwich Man*, inviting new directors including Edward Yang. Contact with these 30-something returned overseas creators led Hou to ponder deeper questions:

**What is real cinema?**

Starting with *The Boys from Fengkuei* (1983), Hou found his answer.

## Revolutionary Film Language: When Machine Serves Human, Not Human Serves Machine

### "Anti-Cinema" Shooting Philosophy

Hou Hsiao-hsien's cinematic revolution centered on one subversive idea: **Make the camera accommodate actors, not actors accommodate the camera.**

This sounds technical but represents a complete cinematic worldview revolution:

**Traditional cinema:** Actor hits mark → Compose shot → Light → Shoot
**Hou cinema:** Actor moves naturally → Camera follows → No artificial lighting → Record

Result: His films have virtually no close-ups, because close-ups require actors to "accommodate" camera positioning.

### Poetics of the Long Take

French *Cahiers du Cinéma* critics described Hou's long takes: "Like the empty spaces in Chinese landscape painting, making time itself the protagonist."

But his long takes differ from Tsai Ming-liang or Angelopoulos—not deliberately "slow," but "objective gazing." He aimed to eliminate camera presence, giving audiences the truest experience closest to daily life.

**Technical Innovations:**
- Minimal rehearsal, letting actors "merge with environment"
- Often no artificial lighting, relying on natural light
- Single scenes shot for half a month until "natural"
- Camera always "recorder," never "director"

> The scene in *Café Lumière* where male and female protagonists pass each other in different trains took nearly half a month to complete. Even German director Wim Wenders called it "incredible."

### Spreading Influence

After watching *The Puppetmaster*, Akira Kurosawa said: "I could never make a film like this."

Contemporary directors influenced by Hou Hsiao-hsien form a stunning list:
- **Jia Zhangke** (Sixth Generation Chinese cinema leader)
- **Hirokazu Kore-eda** (Contemporary Japanese master)
- **Abbas Kiarostami** (Iranian New Wave)

All inherited, in varying degrees, Hou's "objective gaze" and "long-take aesthetics."

## The Venice Miracle: *A City of Sadness* and Taiwan New Cinema's World Conquest

### Seven-Year International Festival Journey (1983-1989)

The Golden Lion for *A City of Sadness* didn't appear from nowhere. From 1983, Hou built international reputation:

| Year | Film | International Awards |
|------|------|---------------------|
| 1983 | *The Boys from Fengkuei* | Nantes Festival Best Film |
| 1984 | *A Summer at Grandpa's* | Nantes Best Film, Locarno Humanitarian Award |
| 1985 | *A Time to Live and a Time to Die* | Berlin FIPRESCI Prize |
| 1986 | *Dust in the Wind* | Nantes Best Music, Cinematography |
| 1989 | *A City of Sadness* | **Venice Golden Lion** |

### Successful International Strategy

*A City of Sadness*'s success was meticulously planned:

1. **Media Offensive**: Producer Qiu Fushen invited major international publication journalists to Taiwan
2. **Cultural Translation**: Elaborate press books with character charts and historical background
3. **Peggy Chiao's Role**: Cultural bridge helping Western audiences understand Eastern aesthetics

> Peggy Chiao: "Don't assume people will naturally understand your film. You must teach them how to read non-Western mainstream film aesthetics."

### Significance of the Turning Point

After *A City of Sadness* won, Taiwan media attitudes toward New Cinema changed 180 degrees—from "box office poison" to "pride of Taiwan."

More importantly, it launched Taiwan cinema's international golden age (1989-1995):
- Edward Yang's *A Brighter Summer Day* (1991 Tokyo Film Festival Special Jury Prize)
- Hou's *The Puppetmaster* (1993 Cannes Jury Prize)
- Ang Lee's *The Wedding Banquet* (1993 Berlin Golden Bear)
- Tsai Ming-liang's *Vive L'Amour* (1994 Venice Golden Lion)

Peggy Chiao described: "Between 1989 and 1995, the most outstanding, most fashionable cinema was Taiwan cinema."

## Artistic Peak: From *The Puppetmaster* to *The Assassin*

### Complete Stylistic Maturity (1990s)

*The Puppetmaster* (1993) is widely considered Hou's artistic pinnacle. This biography of puppeteer Li Tian-lu pushed Hou's aesthetics to extremes:

- **Multilingual coexistence**: Taiwanese, Japanese, Mandarin naturally mixed, reflecting Taiwan's linguistic ecology
- **Theater-within-theater structure**: Boundaries between puppetry and real life blur
- **Minimalist narrative**: Almost no traditional dramatic conflict, relying entirely on atmosphere

Cannes jury member Abbas Kiarostami said this film left him "deeply moved."

### Continued Innovation in the 21st Century

- **flowers of Shanghai** (1998): Adapted from Eileen Chang's novel, entirely in Shanghai dialect
- **Millennium Mambo** (2001): Collaboration with Shu Qi exploring contemporary urban confusion
- **Three Times** (2005): Triptych structure spanning three eras of love

### *The Assassin*: Swan Song (2015)

After seven years in production, *The Assassin* became Hou's cinematic farewell. This adaptation of Tang dynasty legend:

- **Cannes Best Director**: Hou's second major award at European top-tier festivals
- **Ultimate aesthetics**: Called "one of the most visually beautiful films ever made"
- **Cultural depth**: Modern perspective reinterpreting Tang culture

But it was critically acclaimed yet commercially unsuccessful—proving Hou remained an art purist.

## Legacy of the Taiwan New Cinema Godfather

### Levels of Influence

Hou Hsiao-hsien's impact on Taiwan and world cinema operates on three levels:

**Technical**: Pioneered "objective gaze" cinematographic aesthetics
**Cultural**: Proved non-Western cinema could conquer international festivals
**Spiritual**: Insisted on artistic purity, refusing commercial compromise

### Disciples and Heritage

Taiwan directors directly influenced by Hou:
- **Tsai Ming-liang**: Inherited long-take aesthetics, developing more extreme "slow cinema"
- **Ang Lee**: Though Hollywood-bound, Hou's aesthetics visible in films like *Lust, Caution*
- **Edward Yang**: Though stylistically different, shared art cinema ideals

### Continuing International Reputation

Even in retirement, Hou is considered a "living legend" by international cinema:
- **Cinémathèque française** established Hou Hsiao-hsien archive
- **Cannes Film Festival** repeatedly honors his contributions
- **Venice Film Festival** calls him "cinema poet"

> "Hou Hsiao-hsien's films taught the world that cinema can be poetry, not just story." —French *Cahiers du Cinéma*

## Farewell and Eternity (2023 Retirement)

### Alzheimer's Goodbye

In 2023, 76-year-old Hou formally retired due to Alzheimer's disease, shocking international cinema. *The Assassin* became his farewell work.

International media verdict: "End of an era." But his influence will continue forever.

### Greatness in the Ordinary

Retired Hou returned to family life, walking near his Taipei home with his son, living an ordinary elderly man's life. On April 8, 2025, his 78th birthday, media captured heartwarming images of father and son strolling together.

This image is very "Hou Hsiao-hsien"—no dramatics, no close-ups, just quiet, poetic dailiness.

### Place in Cinema History

What Hou ultimately left the world wasn't just films, but an entirely new way of "seeing":

**He proved cinema needn't rely on Hollywood grammar to move the world.**
**He proved "slow" and "quiet" possess equally powerful artistic force.**
**He proved Taiwan could occupy an important position on the international cultural map.**

In an increasingly fast, noisy world, Hou's cinema reminds us: sometimes, the most profound beauty comes from the quietest gaze.

He is Taiwan cinema's pride and world cinema's precious asset. When cinema history is written, Hou Hsiao-hsien's name will shine forever—not because of how many films he made, but because he changed cinema itself.
